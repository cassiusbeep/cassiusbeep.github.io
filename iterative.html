iterative design: Ontra Mobility

Ontra Mobility is a startup who participated in YCombinator S24. our team found Ontra through the demo day listing and were intrigued by their vision of improving public transport systems for the modern day. This is a cause close to all our hearts and we hoped to bring our own perspectives as transit users and designers!

part 1: sketching our ideas back to top

The problem that Ontra seeks to address is the the need to adapt public transport systems to serve wide urban/suburban areas while dealing with huge budget cuts.

Ridership has dropped, but reducing frequency only makes it worse, so Ontra seeks to design high-frequency transit routes and combine them with on-demand ridesharing to serve changing public needs in real time and reduce spending on less-used routes.

Ontra uses data about public transit ridership to optimise the system in real time and suggest improvements to city transport.

It also acts as a navigation tool, planning rider journeys "with the best combination of transit options available." They show a strong focus on integrating on-demand ridesharing platforms with other modes of transport.

My design: Ontra Sketch with different user modes for riders and organisers

  • this sketch combines Ontra's two goals of serving transit routes and providing data insights to city officials.
  • it's not as effective at integrating on-demand services.
  • the user could easily find info for specific transport types
  • missed opportunity: allowing users to access travel stats & updates. not all of the useful data has to be exclusive to admins.
  • the login system might be a nuisance to users in a rush.

Ontra Sketch with rideshare booking

  • this sketch effectively targets Ontra's aim to serve ridesharing requests.
  • it's not as effective at integrating public transport.
  • the bottom navigation bar is very intuitive, but it isn't clear how the home tab would differ from the location pin tab at first glance.
  • really like the clean & simple layout and accessible home-screen map.

Ontra Sketch with travel delay info and route generation

  • this sketch effectively uses Ontra's data-driven approach to improve user experience with the homepage congestion updates.
  • it also shows an integrated journey using both public transport and an on-demand service.
  • the review screen at the end of the journey helps to improve Ontra's data and user confidence in the app.
  • showing one route makes the app feel more assertive and confident as it streamlines the navigation process.
  • the ability to "regenerate" the selected route might make it feel a bit random - maybe we could use a different word or remove the feature.

Ontra Sketch with data collection features and suggestions for late-night transit

  • the conversational style of the navigation makes the app feel friendly.
  • the active tone of the travel suggestion ("you should leave in 15 mins") fits Ontra's aim to provide streamlined, confident routes.
  • this design focuses on collecting user data and using it to improve repeated routes.
  • the data collection might feel intrusive to users and not relevant enough to their immediate needs (the current journey)
  • this design is less successful for users who need to travel to new destinations often.

part 2: building a wireframe back to top

I worked with some team members to build a wireframe incorporating our favourite features into a single design, using Balsamiq Cloud.

    i worked on synthesizing our sketches and feedback into a list of features to keep in our wireframe:

  • homepage congestion info, because it updates users at a glance, as well as the map, for familiarity and quick view of nearby buses etc.
  • integrated rideshare booking, because it serves Ontra's goals
  • clean design and casual/conversational tone
  • option to rate your journey, to provide useful data on Ontra's algorithms
  • night transit mode, as a helpful feature for users out and about who need to plan their journey home
    i also collated the features we disliked across each sketch:

  • removed obtrusive login/data collection prompts, as it can slow down users and feel irrelevant to their task
  • option to "regenerate"/see multiple routes: Ontra's focus is on providing ONE clear path
  • added a way to change journey's start location, in case they're planning ahead
  • added more focus on the transition between public transport & on-demand travel (screen 6)—this is underserved in most travel apps and is a unique selling point of Ontra
  • removed the whole page for night transit mode and made it a push-notification reminder instead (set by the user at the end of a journey)
  • focused more on the user navigation side of Ontra (rather than the city-official-side travel optimisation), to define our scope and include more detail.

Balsamiq Wireframe for Ontra Mobility App (scroll right)

Overall, we aimed for a confident and streamlined navigation tool that efficiently directs users through routes with both public transport and on-demand rideshare services.

part 3: creating a prototype back to top

Before building our high-fidelity prototype, we sought out feedback on our wireframe.

    we found that:

  • our header didn't provide much info. we can remove the "Ontra" title and use the header to give context to the current screen.
  • the delay info on the homepage is not that clear. the colours are memorable but a tutorial or hover tooltip would make them more learnable.
  • we should use the colour coding elsewhere to be cohesive and consistent.
  • we need to clarify what night transit mode does
  • the buttons in the bottom navbar don't make much sense.

I kept all these critiques in mind as I collaborated with the team on our prototype for the app in Figma. The aim of this prototype is to approximate the final app and demonstrate usability for a typical task within Ontra. I was responsible for setting up the layout of the main pages, creating some of the reusable components, and implementing the interactive/navigational elements of our prototype.

    In response to our feedback, we:

  • created more informative titles for each page
  • added similar colour coding to the homepage map as the delay information panel, and added tooltips on press-down to indicate congestion levels
  • we made the decision to remove the page for night transit mode and updated the navbar to match.

    • one bug with our prototype is a known figma issue: sometimes clicking on suggested search results will trigger the delay cards below.

part 4: user testing back to top

Next, we conducted usability tests with our prototype to determine how successful our design would be. We instructed our participants to use our app to navigate from their current location to Waterman Street, and observed/recorded how they tried to complete that task with our interface.

    The typical subtasks in this process were:

  1. Open the Ontra app.
  2. Tap the search box ("Go anywhere")
  3. Read the suggestions and tap the correct destination
  4. Read the suggested route and tap "Let's go"
  5. After this step, users diverged: some felt the objective was completed at this point and stopped the task.
  6. Others read the next screen and continued to reserve a pickup at their station.
  7. They then reached the destination page and left a star rating.
    Some user errors or misunderstandings we observed:

  • When the route was originally served (before clicking "let's go"), some expected that each item on-screen was an alternative transit method and attempted to select one.
  • Users were confused by the "reserve a pickup" option, and weren't sure where they were being picked up from or driven to.
  • It was hard for users to tell how far through the journey they were.

In general, users did well at the start of this task and it matched their expectations. Later in the task, where users had to transition from public transit to ridesharing services, there was more hesitance and confusion.

    Some other user feedback and responses to questions:

  • The map view could be more immersive and descriptive.
  • The interface is overall simple and learnable, especially with clear iconography and calls to action.
  • Users appreciated the conversational tone of the app.
  • Users expected explicit confirmation of ridesharing bookings.
    This was an invaluable exercise for testing our own assumptions of our design and identifying next steps for the process if we were to continue developing an app for Ontra Mobility. Based on these results, we might consider making the following changes to our interface:

  • Making the initial directions clearer/more descriptive, and highlighting transition points between different modes of transit.
  • Clearly marking progress through the user's journey.
  • Adding more visual feedback to the booking process, and a confirmation page.
