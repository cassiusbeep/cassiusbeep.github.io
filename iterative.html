iterative design: Ontra Mobility

Ontra Mobility is a startup who participated in YCombinator S24. Our team found Ontra through the YCombinator demo day listing and were intrigued by their vision of improving public transport systems for the modern day. This is a cause that is close to all our hearts and we hoped to bring our own perspectives as transit users and future tech workers!

This case study will show how we used iterative design processes to develop a prototype for Ontra's mobile app based on their mission.

part 1: sketching our ideas back to top

The problem that Ontra seeks to address is the the need to adapt public transport systems to serve wide urban/suburban areas while dealing with huge budget cuts.

Ridership has dropped, but reducing frequency only makes it worse, so Ontra seeks to design high-frequency transit routes and combine them with on-demand ridesharing to serve changing public needs in real time and reduce spending on less-used routes.

Ontra uses data about public transit ridership to optimise the system in real time and suggest improvements to city transport.

It also acts as a navigation tool, planning rider journeys "with the best combination of transit options available." They show a strong focus on integrating on-demand ridesharing platforms with other modes of transport.

Ontra Sketch with travel delay info and route generation

  • this sketch effectively uses Ontra's data-driven approach to improve user experience with the homepage congestion updates.
  • it also shows an integrated journey using both public transport and an on-demand service.
  • the review screen at the end of the journey helps to improve Ontra's data and user confidence in the app.
  • showing one route makes the app feel more assertive and confident as it streamlines the navigation process.

Ontra Sketch with data collection features and suggestions for late-night transit

  • the conversational style of the navigation makes the app feel friendly.
  • the active tone of the travel suggestion ("you should leave in 15 mins") fits Ontra's aim to provide streamlined, confident routes.
  • this design focuses on collecting user data and using it to improve repeated routes.
  • the data collection might feel intrusive to users and not relevant enough to their immediate needs (the current journey)
  • this design is less successful for users who need to travel to new destinations often.

part 2: building a wireframe back to top

We used our critique of each sketch to create a wireframe incorporating our favourite features into a single design.

Balsamiq Wireframe for Ontra Mobility App (scroll right)

    what we kept from our sketches:

  • homepage congestion info, because it updates users at a glance, as well as the map, for familiarity and quick view of nearby buses etc.
  • integrated rideshare booking, because it serves Ontra's goals
  • clean design and casual/conversational tone
  • option to rate your journey, to provide useful data on Ontra's algorithms
  • night transit mode, as a helpful feature for users out and about who need to plan their journey home
    what we changed from our sketches:

  • removed obtrusive login/data collection prompts, as it can slow down users and feel irrelevant to their task
  • option to "regenerate"/see multiple routes: Ontra's focus is on providing ONE clear path
  • added a way to change journey's start location, in case they're planning ahead
  • added more focus on the transition between public transport & on-demand travel (screen 6)—this is underserved in most travel apps and is a unique selling point of Ontra
  • removed the whole page for night transit mode and made it a push-notification reminder instead (set by the user at the end of a journey)
  • focused more on the user navigation side of Ontra (rather than the city-official-side travel optimisation), to define our scope and include more detail.

Overall, we aimed for a confident and streamlined navigation tool that efficiently directs users through routes with both public transport and on-demand rideshare services.

part 3: creating a prototype back to top

Before building our high-fidelity prototype, we sought out feedback on our wireframe.

    we found that:

  • our header didn't provide much info. we can remove the "Ontra" title and use the header to give context to the current screen.
  • the delay info on the homepage is not that clear. the colours are memorable but a tutorial or hover tooltip would make them more learnable.
  • we should use the colour coding elsewhere to be cohesive and consistent.
  • we need to clarify what night transit mode does
  • the buttons in the bottom navbar don't make much sense.

We kept all these critiques in mind as we created a prototype for the app in Figma. The aim of this prototype is to approximate the final app and demonstrate usability for a typical task within Ontra.
